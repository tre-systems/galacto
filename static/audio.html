The sound

A galaxy, played as music

Galacto's soundscape is not a backing track. It is generated, in real time, from the same simulation you are watching — the gathering mass, the inward collapse, the camera's drift all become sound. This is how the physics is turned into music, and the research on calm, consonance and awe that shaped every choice.

01A soundscape, not a soundtrack

Every sound you hear is synthesized on the fly — oscillators, a noise bed, a code-generated reverb, a feedback delay, a compressor. There are no audio files. Nothing is pre-recorded or looped; if you change the galaxy, the sound changes with it, because the sound is a reading of the galaxy.

Two pieces do the work. A pure-Rust music engine decides what to play: each frame it takes a GalaxyState — the camera, the live controls, and above all the galaxy's own core dynamics — and emits targets for a drone pad, the surrounding texture, and a sparse stream of notes. A Web Audio layer is the how: it owns the node graph and schedules notes ahead on the audio clock so timing stays sample-accurate even when frames stutter. Keeping the musical logic free of any audio dependency means it can be unit-tested like any other pure function — the same discipline the physics uses.

The shape of it

Visuals → GalaxyState → what to play (pure, testable) → how to play it (Web Audio). The generative seed is fixed, so a given run of the galaxy sounds reproducible — and the very same graph renders a finished piece offline, faster than real time.

02What the research says about calm and awe

The two feelings the soundscape aims for — deep calm and a sense of awe — are not guesses. Each rests on a finding from psychoacoustics and affective science, and each maps to a concrete decision in the synthesis:

  • Consonance is the absence of roughness. Plomp & Levelt showed dissonance is the beating of partials that fall too close together inside a critical band [1]. So the pad and starfield are tuned to octaves and fifths, and the scales are consonant — no clustered, beating intervals unless the galaxy is violently colliding.
  • The ear has a tender band at 2–5 kHz. The equal-loudness contours peak there — it is where we hear most acutely [2] — and the sounds people rate most unpleasant cluster in roughly that range [3]. So the sparkle is kept below it, and intermittent (see §05).
  • Breathing near 0.1 Hz settles the nervous system. Slow breathing at about six breaths a minute hits the body's cardiovascular resonance, maximising heart-rate variability and parasympathetic ("rest") tone [4][5]. So the whole bed swells at exactly 0.1 Hz, a passive pacer (§04).
  • Slow tempi lower arousal. The note grid sits near a resting heart rate and never turns frantic (§06).
  • Soft onsets avoid the startle reflex. A sharp, loud attack is an arousal trigger; every note fades in, and the bed has no transients.
  • Awe comes from vastness. Awe is the response to perceived vastness that asks the mind to accommodate it [6]. Sonically that is deep sub-bass, a long cavernous reverb, and a wide stereo field — scale you can hear (§07).
  • No repeating melody. A hook invites you to track and predict it; a non-repeating wander stays, in Eno's phrase for ambient, "as ignorable as it is interesting" [7]. The melody is a slow random walk over the scale, never a tune.
Honest about the claim

These are design heuristics drawn from the literature, applied to a generative art toy — not a clinical or therapeutic instrument. The aim is a soundscape that is pleasant for long, unattended listening and rewards stillness; it is not a medical intervention, and nothing here is a health claim.

03The instrument: layers and signal flow

The bed is a small ensemble of synth voices feeding one master chain, with three parallel sends that paint the surrounding space. The pad and the starfield share a stereo field pan that swings with the camera's orbit; the sub-bass and noise stay centred, so the low end is mono and solid on any system.

The shimmer send is a small trick worth showing. Tapping the pad through a 2x² − 1 transfer curve turns a sine into its octave — a clean frequency doubler — which is then band-passed up into the "air" band and fed to the reverb, for a cosmic sheen that floats over the pad without adding harshness:

04Breathing at six breaths a minute

The single most direct piece of the relaxation research is the breathing pacer. Slow breathing at about six breaths per minute — 0.1 Hz — sits at the body's cardiovascular resonance frequency, where the heartbeat and the breath couple most strongly, driving heart-rate variability and parasympathetic tone to their peak [4][5]. You cannot make someone breathe; but a slow, regular swell in the sound is a cue people unconsciously fall into step with.

So the entire sustained bed rises and falls on a 10-second cycle — a gentle +0.15 swell on top of its level:

The same 0.1 Hz breath is mirrored, in phase, on the master output gain, so the whole mix — not just the bed — quietly inhales and exhales together.

05Staying out of the ear's tender band

Early versions had a "starfield" that sat in the 2–5 kHz region and, over minutes, became a fatiguing whine. That band is exactly where human hearing is most sensitive — the equal-loudness contours dip to their lowest there [2] — and it overlaps the range of the sounds people rate most aversive [3]. A sound can be perfectly in tune and still tire you out simply by living there.

The fix is twofold. First, the twinkling voices are tuned to octaves and fifths well below the band, so they stay consonant and warm:

Second, each star twinkles — every voice rides its own slow LFO and dips to silence rather than holding a steady tone, because a sustained pure tone is far more wearing than an intermittent one. The shimmer sheen is allowed up into the "air" (a band-pass near 2.6 kHz), but as a brief, reverberant glint rather than a continuous presence, so it reads as space, not stridency.

06How the galaxy plays the instrument

What makes it feel alive is that the controls of the instrument are the galaxy's own numbers. A small, throttled GPU read-back (the same one the engineering calls the one sanctioned exception to keeping state on the GPU) returns how much mass has gathered at the core, whether matter is falling in or streaming out, how much it is churning, and whether that motion is organised or random. 07Vastness, and the feeling of awe

Calm is only half of it. The other goal is awe — and awe, the research says, is the response to perceived vastness, something large enough that the mind has to stretch to take it in [6]. There is a sonic vocabulary for vastness: weight at the very bottom, a huge reverberant space, and a wide stereo image. Galacto leans on all three — a deep sub-bass for scale (floored at 36 Hz, below which it is felt by nothing and only muddies the mix), a long convolver reverb, and voices spread hard across the field.

The reverb is generated entirely in code, and its realism comes from one detail: the diffuse tail darkens as it decays, because a real hall absorbs high frequencies faster than low ones. A one-pole filter whose cutoff falls over time does exactly that:

Crucially, the dynamics expand slowly — the breath, the swelling core, layers opening as you zoom — rather than arriving as crescendos. Vastness held calm is the whole point: scale without startle.

08Recording it properly

The same graph that plays live can render a finished piece offline, faster than real time and free of any frame-rate glitches, by replaying a recorded timeline of the galaxy through an OfflineAudioContext. What comes out is then mastered by a pure, native-tested DSP module — no Web Audio involved, so it unit-tests like the physics does.

Mastering means making the piece translate across earbuds, laptops and big speakers alike. It is measured with the broadcast loudness standard (ITU-R BS.1770 K-weighting), normalised to a streaming target, and held under a true-peak ceiling so nothing clips after lossy encoding:

Around that, the chain sums the very low end to mono (so the sub is solid on any system), trims sub-30 Hz rumble, applies raised-cosine fades, and writes a 24-bit WAV. The result is a finished audiovisual piece — the same arc on screen and in the speakers, rendered from a single seed.

09What's evidence-based, and what's taste

As with the physics, it is worth being clear about the line between the research-grounded choices and the aesthetic ones.

Grounded in the literature:

  • consonant tuning to avoid critical-band roughness [1];
  • keeping sustained energy out of the 2–5 kHz fatigue band [2][3];
  • the 0.1 Hz breathing swell at the cardiovascular resonance [4][5];
  • slow tempi and soft note onsets to keep arousal low;
  • sub-bass, long reverb and width to evoke vastness, hence awe [6];
  • a non-repeating melodic wander, in the generative-ambient tradition [7].

Taste, tuned by ear: the specific scale for each scenario, the exact weighting of every galaxy signal onto every parameter, the timbres of the voices, the length and colour of the reverb, and the overall balance. The science says which dials matter; where to set them is a creative choice.

The takeaway

The sound is a real-time reading of the simulation, shaped by what the research says keeps a listener calm and moved. None of it is pre-recorded, and none of it is random noise dressed up — it is the galaxy, made audible.
